Core Thesis

Flowfactory should not merely claim AI governance.

The crowded claim is "we govern AI." The sharper claim is that Flowfactory can make governance a property of the business object itself.

Recommended Positioning

Flowfactory introduces a verifiable trust architecture for AI-native operations: an execution fabric where every operational primitive carries identity, provenance, integrity, and governance metadata.

Why This Is Different

Most vendors govern models, agents, data, or infrastructure. The more interesting Flowfactory territory is the executable business layer: apps, workflows, rules, integrations, approvals, and AI-assisted operational steps.

Human Meaning

The trust paper starts with vulnerability. Enterprise AI increases operational vulnerability because systems can recommend, compose, and execute business actions. Flowfactory can reduce blind vulnerability by making actions inspectable.

Enterprise Meaning

The buyer should not have to ask "do I trust the AI?" The better question is: can I prove what ran, where it came from, who allowed it, and whether it changed?

Strategic Claim

Trust moves from a brand promise into a runtime property. That is defensible only if the product can surface evidence at the object, action, and audit layers.

Alignment With The Trust Paper

From psychological trust to engineered trust.

The paper's useful bridge is that trust is not emotion. It is a decision to accept vulnerability under uncertainty. AI-native operations turn that into a design problem.

Ability: can the system do the work?

In the paper's terms, ability is domain competence. In Flowfactory terms, ability means the primitive declares what it is allowed and designed to do, under what inputs, with which dependencies, and with what known limits.

The product implication: capability metadata cannot be marketing text. It needs runtime-enforceable boundaries: input contracts, data access scope, permitted tools, owner, version, tests, and observed reliability.

Benevolence: whose interest is represented?

Benevolence is difficult for AI because machines do not have moral intention. The enterprise version is mandate alignment: which human, team, policy, or process goal authorized the system to act?

The product implication: every primitive needs a mandate. "This exists to reduce order rework for finance operations" is safer than "AI assistant can optimize workflow." The mandate becomes the reference point for review.

Integrity: does behavior follow acceptable principles?

Integrity maps cleanly to approval paths, version control, change records, policy packs, and tamper evidence. A primitive should not just work; it should be able to show that it is the approved thing, running under approved constraints.

The product implication: signatures, hashes, attestations, policy binding, and immutable audit records are not security garnish. They are how integrity becomes visible.

Calibrated trust: not more trust, better trust.

The goal is not to make users trust AI more. The goal is to make the right level of trust obvious. Overtrust creates automation bias and invisible exposure. Undertrust wastes capability.

The product implication: Flowfactory should expose confidence, limits, approval state, provenance, and exception status in the same operational surface where work happens.

Trust Architecture

The operational primitive becomes the unit of trust.

A primitive can be an app module, workflow step, rule, API connector, AI action, form, approval, transformation, or generated operational component.

What must travel with the primitive

  • Stable identity and human/business ownership.
  • Source, version, generator, dependencies, and change lineage.
  • Cryptographic integrity checks or tamper-evident references.
  • Approval status, policy scope, and runtime permissions.
  • Execution trace and evidence record for each material action.

What this should avoid

  • Anthropomorphic "trust me" language around AI.
  • Governance that exists only as documentation outside execution.
  • Audit logs that explain after the fact but cannot prevent misuse.
  • Generic AI safety claims that cloud and security vendors already own.

Deep Reasoning

The fields that need to be addressed.

These are the minimum conceptual fields I would pressure-test before turning the architecture into external messaging.

1

Primitive ontology

Define exactly what counts as an operational primitive. If it includes apps, rules, workflows, connectors, prompts, agents, approvals, and generated code, each needs a type model and evidence contract.

2

Identity model

Clarify whether identity belongs to the primitive, human owner, AI agent, runtime service, tenant, or all of them. Enterprise trust collapses if identity is ambiguous.

3

Provenance depth

Decide how far back the chain goes: template, AI prompt, generated artifact, human edit, dependency, imported API, test result, approval note. Too shallow is weak; too deep becomes noise.

4

Integrity mechanism

Name the level of proof: hash, signature, certificate, trusted execution, append-only ledger, external notarization, or platform-internal tamper evidence. "Cryptographic" needs specificity.

5

Governance binding

Approval must bind to runtime. Who approved what, for which environment, under which policy, with what expiry and revocation behavior? Otherwise governance is paperwork.

6

Runtime enforcement

Evidence alone is not enough. The architecture should say what happens when a primitive lacks approval, drifts from its signature, exceeds mandate, or calls an unapproved system.

7

Audit and replay

Executives and regulators need reconstructability: what happened, why, under which policy, based on which data, and with which human or AI involvement.

8

Human legibility

The trust signal must be visible without drowning users in metadata. The UI challenge is calibrated trust: enough evidence to guide action, not a security manual on every button.

9

Failure repair

When an AI-assisted workflow fails, the system needs forensic separation: model error, data error, primitive drift, policy exception, approval failure, or integration fault.

Open Questions

What Fredrik and Flowfactory should pressure-test.

These are the questions that turn a strong narrative into a product architecture customers can believe.

What is the smallest demonstrable trust primitive?

Pick one narrow object and make the claim real. Example: a Flowfactory workflow step that has a stable ID, source history, approver, policy scope, signature, runtime validation, and action log. A concrete primitive beats an abstract architecture.

Which evidence should be visible to business users?

The buyer wants proof, but the operator wants clarity. Decide which signals appear in the normal UI: approved, modified, unverified, generated, high-impact, human-reviewed, policy exception, or external dependency.

Where does cryptographic proof actually matter?

Cryptography is powerful but easy to overclaim. Use it where tamper evidence, chain of custody, external verification, or non-repudiation matter. Use simpler governance metadata where cryptography would add complexity without buyer value.

How does this integrate with existing enterprise controls?

The architecture should complement IAM, CI/CD, audit logs, GRC, SIEM, data catalogs, and cloud governance. Flowfactory should not sound like it replaces the security estate; it adds trust semantics at the operational object layer.

What is the buyer pain?

The pain is not "AI is scary." The pain is that AI-native systems make operational composition dynamic, distributed, and harder to prove. Regulated and complex enterprises need evidence before they can scale AI-assisted execution.

What is the killer before/after?

Before: a workflow exists and people hope it is the approved version. After: the workflow can prove its identity, origin, approval state, integrity, runtime scope, and execution history at the moment it acts.
